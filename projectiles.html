Physics in Warfare: Development of Projectiles Through the Ages

Last updated: 19 February 2026

Projectiles

Introduction

Across centuries, the main “physics problem” is not simply making projectiles more powerful. It is reducing sensitivity to uncertainty: controlling how launch variability, wind, drag, and modelling error translate into miss distance.

The history of projectile development is the progressive engineering reduction of system sensitivity.

Smoothbore cannon amplify uncertainty; rifling suppresses aerodynamic instability; fuses and fragmentation redefine accuracy as spatial lethality; bombing exposes system-level open-loop fragility; rockets introduce integration drift; guidance introduces feedback control [1][2][3][4].

The defining transformation is the move from prediction-only systems to correction-capable systems.

1) Early Gunpowder Artillery (14th–18th century)

Smoothbore cannons, round shot, early mortars

Representative devices

  • Culverin (16th–17th century). A long-barrelled smoothbore cannon firing spherical shot. Improved range compared to earlier bombards, but still highly sensitive to windage and powder variation.
  • Napoleonic 12-pounder field gun. Widely used smoothbore artillery piece. Effective at battlefield distances, but dispersion increased rapidly at longer ranges due to spherical drag and velocity variability.
  • Early mortars. High-angle smoothbore weapons firing explosive shells. Range estimation and powder consistency strongly affected impact accuracy.

Gunpowder provided unprecedented impulse energy compared to torsion catapults or trebuchets. But early artillery suffered from extreme inconsistency. A smoothbore cannon firing a spherical ball is sensitive to:

  • variable powder burn rate,
  • inconsistent packing and ignition,
  • windage (gas leakage),
  • barrel fouling altering friction,
  • exit yaw and asymmetry,
  • large aerodynamic drag,
  • crosswind sensitivity,
  • wake instability behind the sphere.

Over long ranges, these small uncertainties compound. Both historical accounts and modern ballistic reconstruction confirm substantial dispersion in black-powder artillery [2][5].

What changed historically

The key change was not simply more energy — it was the recognition that energy alone does not guarantee effectiveness. Benjamin Robins’ ballistic pendulum experiments demonstrated that muzzle velocity was neither constant nor easily predictable [6]. Robins also recognised the importance of air resistance, something earlier gunnery tables often ignored. This marks the beginning of scientific exterior ballistics.

Deeper physics: internal ballistics

Projectile acceleration inside the barrel

\[ m\,\frac{dv}{dt} \;=\; A\,P(t)\;-\;F_f \]

Variables

  • \(m\) = projectile mass
  • \(v\) = projectile velocity in the barrel
  • \(t\) = time
  • \(A\) = bore cross-sectional area
  • \(P(t)\) = time-dependent gas pressure
  • \(F_f\) = friction force in barrel

Muzzle velocity (impulse form)

\[ v_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{m}\int_{0}^{t_{\text{exit}}} A\,P(t)\,dt \]
  • \(v_0\) = muzzle velocity
  • \(t_{\text{exit}}\) = time when the projectile exits the barrel

Black powder combustion is not perfectly uniform. Variations in grain geometry and packing produce pressure-curve differences. If the pressure deviates by \(\Delta P(t)\), then the corresponding muzzle-velocity deviation is:

\[ \Delta v_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{m}\int_{0}^{t_{\text{exit}}} A\,\Delta P(t)\,dt \]

Thus, velocity spread is directly proportional to combustion variability [1]. Windage reduces effective chamber pressure and can produce asymmetric acceleration, introducing yaw at muzzle exit [5].

Error amplification

Vacuum range (fixed launch angle, no drag)

\[ R \;\propto\; \frac{v_0^2}{g} \]

Sensitivity to muzzle-velocity error

\[ \frac{\Delta R}{R} \;\approx\; 2\,\frac{\Delta v_0}{v_0} \]

This quadratic mapping means velocity error is amplified in range [1].

Deeper physics: aerodynamic instability

Quadratic drag model (vector form)

\[ m\,\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} \;=\; m\,\mathbf{g}\;-\;\frac{1}{2}\,\rho\,C_D\,A\,\lVert \mathbf{v}\rVert\,\mathbf{v} \]

Variables

  • \(\rho\) = air density
  • \(C_D\) = drag coefficient
  • \(A\) = reference area
  • \(\mathbf{v}\) = velocity vector
  • \(\lVert \mathbf{v}\rVert\) = speed
  • \(\mathbf{g}\) = gravitational acceleration vector

Sphere (subsonic regime)

\[ C_D \;\approx\; 0.47 \]

[3]

Ballistic coefficient

\[ BC \;=\; \frac{m}{C_D\,A} \]

Low \(BC\) means strong deceleration. In a simplified speed-only model:

\[ \frac{dV}{dt} \;=\; -\frac{\rho}{2\,BC}\,V^2 \]

Wind drift scaling

\[ \Delta y \;=\; \int_{0}^{t_{\text{flight}}} v_{\text{wind}}(t)\,dt \]

Additionally, spherical projectiles exhibit unstable wake separation at moderate Reynolds number, causing fluctuating drag forces [3]. Thus smoothbores combine internal pressure variability, aerodynamic drag instability, and long atmospheric exposure: high energy, high uncertainty.

Section summary

Early gunpowder artillery achieved unprecedented impulse energy but lacked control over variability. Combustion inconsistency, windage, spherical drag, and long time-of-flight amplified small launch differences into large dispersion. The physics of the era was dominated by sensitivity growth rather than precision engineering.

Example

In 17th-century siege warfare campaigns in Europe, smoothbore cannon were effective against static walls but unreliable at long-range targeting of moving formations. The spherical round shot, combined with inconsistent powder charges, meant artillery was used more for area suppression than precise engagement [2][5].

2) Rifling and Elongated Shells (19th century)

Spin stabilisation and the predictable projectile

Representative devices

  • Armstrong Rifle (1850s–1860s). One of the first successful rifled breech-loading artillery systems. Demonstrated dramatically improved range and accuracy through spin stabilisation.
  • Parrott Rifle (American Civil War). Rifled artillery piece using elongated shells. Showed improved ballistic performance over smoothbores but still limited by metallurgy.
  • Minié Ball (rifled musket projectile). Conical bullet designed to expand into rifling grooves. Increased accuracy and range compared to spherical musket balls.

Rifling adds controlled angular momentum to the projectile. Instead of tumbling chaotically, the projectile stabilises gyroscopically. This suppresses aerodynamic orientation error and dramatically reduces dispersion. It also allows elongated projectiles with lower drag.

What changed historically

Two major engineering developments occurred:

  • spiral rifling grooves,
  • ogival (streamlined) projectile shapes.

This increased range, reduced yaw, and enabled reliable ballistic tables [2]. Instead of fighting aerodynamic instability, rifling prevents it.

Deeper physics: gyroscopic stability

Angular momentum

\[ \mathbf{L} \;=\; I\,\omega\,\hat{\mathbf{s}} \]
  • \(\mathbf{L}\) = angular momentum vector
  • \(I\) = moment of inertia about the spin axis
  • \(\omega\) = spin angular velocity
  • \(\hat{\mathbf{s}}\) = unit vector along spin axis

Aerodynamic torque (scaled form)

\[ \tau \;\sim\; q\,S\,d\,C_{m_\alpha} \]
  • \(\tau\) = aerodynamic pitching torque magnitude
  • \(q\) = dynamic pressure
  • \(S\) = reference area
  • \(d\) = projectile diameter
  • \(C_{m_\alpha}\) = pitching-moment coefficient slope (per angle of attack)

Dynamic pressure

\[ q \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\rho\,V^2 \]
  • \(\rho\) = air density
  • \(V\) = speed magnitude

Gyroscopic stability factor (scaled form)

\[ S_g \;=\; \frac{I\,\omega}{q\,S\,d\,C_{m_\alpha}} \]
  • \(S_g\) = gyroscopic stability factor

If \(S_g > 1\), the projectile is stable [1]. Spin causes applied torque to produce precession rather than tumbling. However, spin introduces secondary effects:

  • yaw of repose,
  • Magnus drift (spin-induced lateral force).

Thus rifling reduces instability but introduces predictable spin-drift corrections.

Ballistic coefficient improvements

Elongated projectiles reduce drag coefficient and increase ballistic coefficient:

\[ BC \;=\; \frac{m}{C_D\,A} \]

Higher \(BC\) reduces deceleration in a simplified speed-only model:

\[ \frac{dV}{dt} \;=\; -\frac{\rho}{2\,BC}\,V^2 \]

Reduced time-of-flight reduces wind sensitivity. Thus rifling reduces both rotational and aerodynamic uncertainty. This is the first major engineered suppression of system sensitivity.

Section summary

Rifling marked the first major reduction in aerodynamic uncertainty. By imparting spin, projectiles became gyroscopically stable, eliminating tumbling and dramatically reducing drag variability. Elongated shells increased ballistic coefficient and shortened time-of-flight, reducing wind sensitivity.

Example

The introduction of rifled artillery in the mid-19th century, such as the Armstrong gun, significantly increased effective range and accuracy during conflicts like the Crimean War. Rifled guns could engage targets at distances that smoothbores could not reliably reach [2].

3) High Explosives, Fuses, and Fragmentation

Before high explosives, lethality required direct impact. With explosive filling, lethality becomes spatial. Effectiveness depends on burst height, fragment velocity, fragment density, and lethal radius. Accuracy becomes probabilistic rather than purely geometric.

Representative devices

  • Mills Bomb (No. 36 grenade, WWI). Fragmentation grenade designed with a segmented casing. Demonstrates controlled fragment production and spatial lethality rather than precision impact.
  • WWII artillery airburst shells. Timed-fuze shells designed to detonate above ground, maximising fragment distribution across trenches or infantry formations.
  • German S-mine (“Bouncing Betty”). Anti-personnel mine that launched upward before detonating, optimising fragment spread at torso height.

Fuse timing uncertainty

Timed fuses introduce their own uncertainty. If burst timing error is \(\Delta t\), then the burst height error is approximately:

\[ \Delta h \;\approx\; V\,\Delta t \]
  • \(\Delta h\) = burst height error
  • \(V\) = shell vertical speed at burst (approx.)
  • \(\Delta t\) = fuze timing error

Height variation changes fragment density at ground level, so even explosive shells remain sensitive to timing precision.

Deeper physics: Gurney velocity

A standard scaling for fragment velocity from an explosive casing is the Gurney model:

\[ V_f \;=\; \sqrt{2E \,\frac{M}{C+M}} \]

[7]

  • \(V_f\) = characteristic fragment velocity
  • \(E\) = Gurney energy (explosive energy per unit mass, model parameter)
  • \(M\) = explosive mass
  • \(C\) = casing mass

Fragment kinetic energy

\[ E_k \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,m_f\,V_f^2 \]
  • \(E_k\) = kinetic energy of a fragment
  • \(m_f\) = fragment mass

Energy scales quadratically with velocity, so modest changes in \(V_f\) can significantly change lethality.

Fragment mass distribution

A commonly used empirical model for fragment mass distribution is the Mott distribution:

\[ N(m) \;=\; N_0 \exp\!\left(-\sqrt{\frac{m}{m_0}}\right) \]

[8]

  • \(N(m)\) = expected number of fragments with mass \(\ge m\) (model-dependent convention)
  • \(N_0\) = total-fragment scaling parameter
  • \(m_0\) = characteristic fragment-mass parameter

Fragment spatial density decreases approximately as an inverse-square spreading:

\[ n(r) \;\propto\; \frac{1}{r^2} \]
  • \(n(r)\) = fragment areal density at radius \(r\)
  • \(r\) = distance from burst point

Lethal probability depends on both fragment velocity and areal density. This reframes “accuracy” as volumetric lethality: the question becomes “what fraction of a region receives sufficient energy deposition?” rather than “did we hit a point?”

Section summary

High explosives transformed accuracy from a purely geometric concept to a spatial energy-distribution problem. Fragment velocity, fragment density, and burst height became as important as impact location. Lethality became probabilistic rather than point-based.

Example

World War I and II artillery frequently used airburst shells to maximise fragment spread over trench systems. Even if a shell did not strike directly, the fragmentation cloud could cause significant casualties. Effectiveness depended on burst timing and fragment dispersion, not just impact coordinates [7][8].

4) Unguided Aerial Bombing (WWII baseline)

Open-loop delivery and system-level uncertainty

A bomb dropped from an aircraft is just another projectile, but one released at high altitude and high horizontal velocity. When the bomb leaves the aircraft, it already has the forward velocity of the aircraft, a specific release height, a specific release time, and a specific orientation. From that moment onward, its motion is entirely governed by gravity, aerodynamic drag, and atmospheric wind. There is no mechanism for correction after release.

This makes the system open-loop. Open-loop means the bomb does not measure its own position or correct its trajectory: everything must be calculated correctly before it is dropped. In theory, if you know aircraft speed, aircraft altitude, air density, wind velocity profile, and drag coefficient, you can predict where the bomb will land.

In practice, each of these quantities contains uncertainty: wind speed changes with altitude (wind shear), air density varies with temperature and pressure, aircraft pitch and roll introduce small release-angle deviations, the release mechanism has timing tolerances, and human operators must estimate and input environmental parameters. Because the bomb may fall for 20–40 seconds from high altitude, small errors accumulate over a long time-of-flight. Even a small crosswind estimation error can translate into hundreds of metres of lateral displacement.

Representative devices

  • Mk 82 General Purpose Bomb (unguided). A free-fall bomb relying entirely on aircraft release conditions and atmospheric modelling.
  • British 500 lb and 1000 lb WWII bombs. Used in strategic bombing campaigns. Their dispersion demonstrated open-loop sensitivity to wind and release error.
  • V-1 Flying Bomb (pulsejet, unguided). While powered, it lacked terminal guidance. Its accuracy was limited by navigation error and engine cutoff estimation.

System-level uncertainty

WWII bombing revealed additional uncertainty sources: crosswind shear with altitude, density stratification, aircraft roll and yaw, bomb release timing lag, and human estimation. The USSBS reported only about 20% of bombs fell within 1000 ft of the aim point [9]. This is statistical evidence of open-loop fragility.

Deeper physics

Equation of motion with wind-relative drag (vector form)

\[ m\,\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} \;=\; m\,\mathbf{g} \;-\; \frac{1}{2}\,\rho\,C_D\,A\, \left\lVert \mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}_{\text{wind}} \right\rVert \left(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}_{\text{wind}}\right) \]
  • \(m\) = bomb mass
  • \(\mathbf{v}\) = bomb velocity vector
  • \(\mathbf{g}\) = gravitational acceleration vector
  • \(\rho\) = air density
  • \(C_D\) = drag coefficient
  • \(A\) = reference area
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{wind}}\) = wind velocity vector (generally altitude-dependent)
  • \(\lVert \cdot \rVert\) = vector magnitude

Wind drift (cross-range displacement)

\[ \Delta y \;=\; \int_{0}^{t_{\text{impact}}} v_{\text{wind}}\!\big(z(t),t\big)\,dt \]
  • \(\Delta y\) = cross-range drift
  • \(t_{\text{impact}}\) = time of impact
  • \(v_{\text{wind}}(z(t),t)\) = crosswind component as a function of altitude (and possibly time)

If the wind estimate has an error \(\delta w\) (crosswind component), then a first-order drift error scaling is:

\[ \delta y \;\approx\; t_{\text{flight}}\,\delta w \]
  • \(\delta y\) = cross-range miss due to wind estimation error
  • \(t_{\text{flight}}\) = bomb time-of-flight
  • \(\delta w\) = crosswind estimation error

Time-of-flight multiplies uncertainty. Bombing demonstrates environmental sensitivity dominating mechanical precision.

Section summary

Unguided bombing exposed the fragility of open-loop prediction in complex environments. Even with advanced bomb sights, atmospheric uncertainty, release timing, and aircraft motion produced large dispersion.

Example

The United States Strategic Bombing Survey reported that during WWII, only about 20% of bombs fell within 1000 feet of the aiming point under operational conditions [9]. This empirical result demonstrated that environmental and human uncertainties dominated ballistic prediction.

5) Rockets and Ballistic Missiles

Powered flight, staging, and accumulating navigation error

Rockets differ fundamentally from artillery and bombs because propulsion continues after launch. A cannon imparts velocity once, and the projectile coasts. A rocket, by contrast, accelerates for an extended period during powered flight.

This has two major consequences:

  • Range increases dramatically.
  • Errors accumulate during thrust.

If the thrust direction is slightly misaligned, or if thrust magnitude varies slightly from expectation, the velocity error grows over the entire burn time. Because velocity determines future position, small early deviations can become large trajectory differences. Unlike artillery, where uncertainty is mostly “set” at launch, rockets remain dynamically sensitive while the engine is burning.

This is a system-level change in where uncertainty enters. In a cannon, the main random variables are muzzle velocity, departure angle, and the external atmosphere. In a rocket, additional uncertainty enters continuously during flight: thrust magnitude and thrust direction are time-dependent, and the vehicle’s mass is changing as propellant is expended. Any mismatch between the assumed burn profile and the real burn profile accumulates into a trajectory deviation.

Additionally, long-range rockets rely on inertial navigation systems. These systems measure acceleration and integrate it to determine velocity and position. But integration amplifies small sensor biases over time. A tiny accelerometer offset can grow into substantial position error after several minutes of flight. Historically, early ballistic missiles such as the V-2 demonstrated that powered flight allowed unprecedented range, but accuracy was limited by guidance precision rather than propulsion energy.

Representative devices

  • V-2 Rocket (Germany, WWII). First long-range ballistic missile. Demonstrated powered ascent followed by ballistic trajectory. Accuracy limited by inertial guidance precision.
  • R-7 Semyorka (early ICBM). Early Soviet intercontinental ballistic missile. Accuracy constrained by inertial navigation drift.
  • Scud missile (Cold War era). Short-range ballistic missile with significant circular error probable due to guidance limitations.

Rocket equation

\[ \Delta v \;=\; v_e \,\ln\!\left(\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right) \]

[10]

  • \(\Delta v\) = total achievable change in velocity (“delta-v”)
  • \(v_e\) = effective exhaust velocity
  • \(m_0\) = initial mass (with propellant)
  • \(m_f\) = final mass (after burn)

Staging increases achievable \(\Delta v\) by repeatedly resetting \(m_0/m_f\): after each stage is exhausted and dropped, the remaining vehicle mass is reduced, enabling a new high mass ratio for the next burn. This is the engineering route to intercontinental range, but it also creates additional sensitivity: separation timing, stage ignition transients, and guidance handover between phases can all inject error.

Powered ascent

\[ m\,\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} \;=\; \mathbf{T}(t) \;+\; m\,\mathbf{g} \;-\; \mathbf{D} \]
  • \(\mathbf{T}(t)\) = thrust vector (time-dependent)
  • \(\mathbf{D}\) = aerodynamic drag force vector
  • \(\mathbf{g}\) = gravitational acceleration vector
  • \(m\) = vehicle mass (decreasing during burn)

A small thrust-direction misalignment \(\Delta\theta\) produces a growing off-axis velocity error during burn because thrust acts for an extended time. A useful scaling is:

\[ \Delta v_{\perp} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{m}\int_{0}^{t_b} T(t)\,\sin(\Delta\theta)\,dt \;\approx\; \sin(\Delta\theta)\,\Delta v \]
  • \(\Delta v_{\perp}\) = lateral (off-axis) velocity error accumulated during burn
  • \(t_b\) = burn time
  • \(T(t)\) = thrust magnitude
  • \(\Delta\theta\) = thrust-direction error

This matters because position error is roughly the time-integral of velocity error: an early \(\Delta v_{\perp}\) can map into a large miss distance by the time the vehicle reaches the ballistic phase. In other words, powered flight does not merely “add energy” — it adds a long window over which small directional errors can integrate into large trajectory divergence.

Inertial navigation drift

Inertial systems estimate position by double integration of measured acceleration:

\[ x(t) \;=\; \int_{0}^{t}\left(\int_{0}^{\tau} a(\tau')\,d\tau'\right)d\tau \]

If the accelerometer has a constant bias \(b\) (so \(a_{\text{meas}} = a_{\text{true}} + b\)), then the induced position error grows as:

\[ x_{\text{error}}(t) \;\approx\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,b\,t^2 \]

Error grows quadratically with time [11]. This is the key mechanism behind long-range missile dispersion: even when aerodynamics and propulsion are repeatable, guidance is an integration process, and integration turns small bias into large displacement.

The consequence is that rockets shift dominant uncertainty away from muzzle variability and toward navigation drift, burn profile mismatch, and attitude-control error. In this regime, “better engineering” means better sensors, better calibration, better control authority, and better modelling of how thrust and mass evolve with time.

Section summary

Rockets extend propulsion over time, greatly increasing range but allowing errors to accumulate during powered flight. Long-range missiles also depend on inertial navigation, where small sensor biases double-integrate into large position errors. The dominant uncertainty shifts from “what happened at launch” to “how errors grow during burn and integration.”

Example

The German V-2 rocket demonstrated both the power and the limitation of early ballistic missiles. It achieved unprecedented range for its time (hundreds of kilometres) via powered ascent followed by ballistic flight, but operational accuracy was limited primarily by inertial guidance. Small gyroscope drift, thrust misalignment, and accelerometer bias accumulated over the multi-minute trajectory, producing kilometre-scale terminal dispersion under real conditions [10][11].

This same mechanism constrained early Cold War ballistic missiles. Systems such as the R-7 and Scud could deliver payloads over very large distances, but circular error probable remained large relative to modern standards. Even when propulsion performed nominally, small attitude-control and navigation-integration errors accumulated over burn time and flight time, making accuracy a guidance-and-sensor problem rather than an energy problem. The historical shift is clear: once you can reach the target, the dominant question becomes how well you can measure and control the state throughout flight.

6) Precision-Guided Munitions

Feedback control is the true discontinuity

Precision-guided munitions represent the fundamental shift in projectile physics: the introduction of feedback. Earlier systems—artillery, bombs, rockets—relied on prediction. Engineers calculated a trajectory, launched the projectile, and hoped that environmental conditions matched the model.

Guided systems operate differently. They continuously measure their position, compare it to a desired target location, and apply corrective forces during flight. Small deviations caused by wind, drag variation, or release error are detected and reduced rather than allowed to grow. This closes the loop.

Instead of predict → launch → hope, the system becomes measure → compare → correct → repeat. Because corrections are applied in real time, sensitivity to disturbances is dramatically reduced. Wind no longer produces large drift if it can be compensated mid-flight. Release error is no longer final if control surfaces can adjust the trajectory. The defining change is not more power or better aerodynamics, but active error suppression.

Representative devices

  • Paveway laser-guided bomb. Uses laser designation and control fins to correct trajectory mid-flight. Demonstrates closed-loop correction.
  • JDAM (Joint Direct Attack Munition). GPS-guided bomb kit converting unguided bombs into precision weapons. Reduces wind and release sensitivity.
  • AGM-114 Hellfire missile. Laser-guided missile with active control surfaces for continuous trajectory correction.

Deeper physics

The key difference between guided and unguided systems is the addition of a control force. Without guidance, motion is governed by external forces only:

\[ m\,\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} \]

With guidance:

\[ m\,\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} \;+\; \mathbf{F}_{\text{control}} \]
  • \(m\) = projectile mass
  • \(\mathbf{r}\) = position vector
  • \(\ddot{\mathbf{r}}\) = acceleration
  • \(\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}\) = gravity, drag, wind, etc.
  • \(\mathbf{F}_{\text{control}}\) = force from control fins, thrust vectoring, etc.

Simple feedback control law

A minimal proportional–derivative (PD) control model for commanded control acceleration is:

\[ \mathbf{a}_{\text{control}} \;=\; k\,(\mathbf{r}_{\text{target}} - \mathbf{r}) \;-\; c\,\mathbf{v} \]
  • \(\mathbf{a}_{\text{control}}\) = control acceleration command
  • \(k\) = proportional gain
  • \(c\) = damping coefficient
  • \(\mathbf{r}\) = current position
  • \(\mathbf{r}_{\text{target}}\) = desired target position
  • \(\mathbf{v}=\dot{\mathbf{r}}\) = velocity

The first term pulls the projectile toward the target. The second term damps motion to prevent oscillation or overshoot.

Closed-loop equation

Substituting control into the dynamics gives a standard closed-loop form (schematic):

\[ m\,\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;+\; c\,\dot{\mathbf{r}} \;+\; k\,\mathbf{r} \;=\; k\,\mathbf{r}_{\text{target}} \;+\; \mathbf{d}(t) \]
  • \(\mathbf{d}(t)\) = disturbances (wind, modelling error, etc.)

This equation shows that position error is actively driven toward zero rather than passively accumulating.

Sensitivity reduction

Open-loop systems allow disturbances to accumulate over time. In closed-loop systems, sensitivity is reduced approximately as:

\[ \text{sensitivity}_{\text{closed}} \;\approx\; \frac{\text{sensitivity}_{\textopen}}{1 + G_{\text{loop}}} \]
  • \(G_{\text{loop}}\) = loop gain (set by controller gain and system response)

Higher loop gain reduces disturbance amplification.

Section summary

Precision-guided munitions introduced feedback control, fundamentally altering projectile physics. By continuously measuring position and correcting trajectory, guided systems actively suppress disturbance growth instead of merely predicting it.

Example

Laser-guided bombs used in conflicts from the Vietnam War onward reduced circular error probable dramatically compared to unguided bombs. Wind and release variability were corrected mid-flight rather than passively endured. This represents the defining transformation: from open-loop ballistic prediction to closed-loop control.
