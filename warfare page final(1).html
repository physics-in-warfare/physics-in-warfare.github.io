The Mechanics of the Middle Ages: Classical Physics on the Battlefield

18 February 2026 | 17:05

Introduction

The medieval period, also known as the middle ages, spanned roughly a thousand years from the fall of the Western Roman Empire in the 5th century to the dawn of the Renaissance in the late 15th century. Contrary to its common perception as a dark period in history, this era came as a millennium of rapid mechanical evolution. The Early Middle Ages carried on the concepts of Roman fortifications and basic infantry tactics, which were improved upon drastically in the High Middle Ages. This period saw a revolution in defensive masonry and the construction of imposing, near-indestructible fortresses and castles, and the perfection of torsion-powered catapults. By the Late Middle Ages, the development of the counterweight trebuchet and the refinement of plate armour saw the height of mechanical, structural and material physics applications, heralding the beginning of the age of chemical propellants.

1. The Mechanics of Momentum and Impact (c. 500 – 1000)

The Early Middle Ages was defined by a transition from Roman military tactics to the rise of feudal heavy cavalry. The physics of this period focused on the efficient transfer of energy through direct impact and simple mechanisms.

The Mounted Knight and Linear Momentum

Source: medievalart.org

The introduction of the stirrup around the year 800 [1] allowed a rider to deliver powerful strikes to opponents by channeling the linear momentum ($p$) of a charging horse through a lance tip, where

$p = mv$ [2]

relates the combined mass $m$ of the horse and rider and the velocity of the gallop $v$. The force delivered by the lance strike is then given as:

$F = \frac{dp}{dt}$ [3]

where $dp/dt$ is the rate of change of momentum of the horse and rider. A combined mass of over 700 kg moving at up to 30 km/h could subsequently deliver forces in excess of 150,000 Newtons, enough to take out any infantry caught by the strike.

Siege Tactics and Inertia

Source: etc.usf.edu

Early siege engines such as the mangonel utilised torsion mechanisms to store elastic potential energy ($EPE$) by winding ropes to draw back a sling, and then releasing all the tension to convert it into the potential energy of a projectile. Battering rams utilised the principle of inertia to break through sturdy wooden and metal gates, where Newton's second law,

$F = ma$ [4]

relates the large mass $m$ of the ram and its acceleration $a$ as it is swung forwards. By maximising $m$ it was possible to deliver powerful impact forces.

Friction on the Battlefield

The Battle of Tours (~732 AD) [5] demonstrated how environmental physics, specifically friction, could neutralise heavy infantry. Muddy, uneven terrain increases the coefficient of kinetic friction $\mu_k$ in the equation

$F = \mu_k N$ [6]

where $N$ is the normal force acting on the soldier, the reaction force to their weight acting downwards. Considering the already increased weight of an armoured knight, increasing the coefficient of friction meant knights had to overcome large frictional forces simply to advance through the battlefield. As the attacking forces relied on the rapid advance of cavalry and infantry, the frictional forces of the battlefield hindered their assault and allowed the defending Frankish troops to methodically take down the attackers.

2. Tension, Work, and Stored Energy (c. 1066 – 1215)

As fortifications and defenses grew stronger, missile ballistics evolved to maximise the energy stored in the weapon itself before release.

The Ballistics of Bows

The Work-Energy principle was central in the rivalry between the longbow and the crossbow. It relates the work done ($W$) by a system to the force ($F$) and displacement ($d$) by

$W = F \cdot d$ [7]

The longbow maximised draw length using light, flexible yew wood, a naturally composite material. The outer sapwood is flexible enough to handle high tension, whilst the inner heartwood handles compression of the bow. By contrast, the crossbow used mechanical devices to pull back the drawstring, maximising the draw weight ($F$) over a much shorter draw length.

Potential Energy and High Ground

At the Battle of Hastings (1066) [8], the use of high ground offered a distinct advantage in Gravitational Potential Energy, where

$GPE = mgh$ [9]

Projectiles fired from an elevation $h$ gain kinetic energy as they descend, increasing their terminal velocity compared to those fired from ground level. Conversely, this same principle meant that the attacking forces, weighed down with heavy armour and weaponry (increasing their effective mass $m$) had to exert greater force to reach the troops at high ground.

Structural Equilibrium

In 1215 at the Siege of Rochester [10], engineers utilised a technique known as undermining to collapse castle towers. By removing stone from load-bearing walls, they shifted the centre of gravity of the structure, disrupting its static equilibrium and causing it to come crashing down, causing chaos within the castle and allowing the attacking troops to charge in.

3. Gravitational Potential and Structural Engineering (c.1180 - 1415)

This period in the High Middle Ages represents the apex of mechanical siege warfare and defensive masonry, as the former was forced to evolve in response to developments in the latter.

The Counterweight Trebuchet

Introduced around the year 1180 [11], this engine was a powerful machine that masterfully converted the $GPE$ of a falling counterweight into Rotational Kinetic Energy.

$K = \frac{1}{2} I\omega^2$ [12]

describes the relation between the rotational kinetic energy $K$, the moment of inertia $I$ and the angular velocity $\omega$. With counterweights weighing in the region of 10 to 30 tons, an immense amount of energy could be transferred to the stone projectiles as per Eq. $9$, annihilating any infantry or defenses caught in the blast. The addition of a sling at the end of the long lever arm creates a second pivot point, allowing the projectile to travel in a larger arc than that afforded by the arm itself. Since the release velocity is defined by

$v = \omega r$ [13]

where $r$ is the radius from the main pivot point to the projectile, the increased effective radius released the projectiles with even greater velocity.

Compression and Tension in Castle Masonry

Castle designs evolved from square structures with sharp corners to rounded towers because stone is incredibly strong under compression but weak under tension, thanks to the densely packed atoms that resist being squeezed but are prone to propagating cracks and snapping. By designing rounded castle walls, impact forces were distributed across the entire structure rather than onto a single weak corner where tension would cause the stone to crack and the wall to fall. These concentric castle designs are demonstrated particularly well in the likes of Château Gaillard [14], considered a masterpiece of its time.

4. Terminal Ballistics and the Dawn of Chemical Propellants (c. 1326 – 1500)

Pressure and Penetration

To defeat the more advanced white plate armour [15] of the Late Middle Ages, weapons had to maximise their applied pressure ($P$). A sword slash distributes its force ($F$) over a relatively large area ($A$), resulting in low pressure. By comparison, a bodkin arrow, spear point or lance concentrates that same force into a significantly smaller area, creating the high pressure necessary to pierce steel.

$P = \frac{F}{A}$ [16]

The goal of maximising pressure was to exceed the yield strength of the steel plate. While plate armour was designed to deflect energy through its curved shape, a direct hit from a bodkin arrow concentrated several hundred Joules of energy into a point of less than $1 \text{ mm}^2$. This created a localized stress concentration that caused the steel to undergo plastic deformation, allowing the arrow to breach the protective layer.

Pressure Distribution of Edged vs. Pointed Weapons. This model illustrates the concentration of a 500N force across a portion of a sword blade and the point of an arrow respectively. For the sword (left), force is distributed along a 50mm linear edge, resulting in a broad pressure ridge [20, 23]. Conversely, the bodkin arrow (right) focuses the identical force into a point-impact ($r = 0.1$mm), creating a pressure spike that enables it to penetrate armour by exceeding the material's yield strength [21, 24].

Inertia and Blunt Force

When plate armour became too thick to pierce with conventional weapons, soldiers turned to maces and war hammers. These weapons utilised inertia and the transfer of momentum to deliver kinetic energy through the armour straight to the target. Even if the armour did not break, the energy was transmitted to the wearer's body, causing internal trauma without needing to penetrate the material. These weapons tended to maximise mass $m$ and/or surface area $A$, taking advantage of Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$) and the above pressure equation to deliver greater forces.

The Chemical Shift

The appearance of the first cannons in Europe around the year 1326 [17] signaled the end of the mechanical age, as warfare moved from storing energy in wood and rope to releasing the chemical potential energy stored in gunpowder, capable of shattering even the strongest round stone towers and tearing through infantry. Conclusion

From the destructive use of gravitational potential energy in trebuchet counterweights to the weaponized inertia of close-quarters combat, the medieval period demonstrates the continuous application of classical mechanics to gain battlefield dominance. The transition from stored mechanical energy to chemical propellants marked the end of an era and the birth of modern ballistics.
